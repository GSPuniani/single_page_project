Primer on F1

Due to its high-risk nature, F1 is prone to many collisions, some of which can be fatal. In fact, astronaut Scott Kelly observed that F1 drivers are about as likely to die from occupational hazards as astronauts.

Recommended Viewing

The 2020 season is currently in session after a delay due to the COVID-19 pandemic, so races can be streamed on ESPN+ or other services that offer live sports. A great documentary series chronicling the 2018 and 2019 seasons is currently available on Netflix; it is called F1: Drive to Survive. There are also other documentary films about key figures in F1 history, such as the film Senna about legendary driver Ayrton Senna. Additionally, there is a fantastic Hollywood film called Rush from director Ron Howard about the rivalry between drivers James Hunt and Nikki Lauda in the 1970's.

F1 Racecar Technology

Modern F1 cars are designed, in a way, to be kind of like the opposite of aircraft. Formula 1 cars attain such incredibly high speeds that the they could generate their own lift force, like airplanes speeding up before takeoff. Thus, F1 engineers design the cars so that the rear wings generate "downward lift" so that the car maintains grip on the track.

Circuits

Formula 1 races take place all over the world. In fact, there is at least one F1 circuit on 6 of the 7 continents of the world (no races are held in Antarctica).
